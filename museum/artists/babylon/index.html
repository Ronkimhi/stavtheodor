Babylon

capital city of Babylonia and an archaeological site in modern-day Iraq, 1894–539 BCE

Babylon was an ancient city located on the lower Euphrates river in southern Mesopotamia, within modern-day Hillah, Iraq, about 85 kilometres south of modern-day Baghdad. Babylon functioned as the main cultural and political centre of the Akkadian-speaking region of Babylonia. Its rulers established two important empires in antiquity, the 19th–16th century BC Old Babylonian Empire, and the 7th–6th century BC Neo-Babylonian Empire. Babylon was also used as a regional capital of other empires, such as the Achaemenid Empire.

Brick structures in Babylon, 2016. Babylon was an ancient city located on the lower Euphrates river in southern Mesopotamia, within modern-day Hillah, Iraq, about 85 kilometres south of modern-day Baghdad. Babylon functioned as the main cultural and political centre of the Akkadian-speaking region of Babylonia.

The earliest attested mention of Babylon dates to the late 3rd millennium BC, during the reign of the Akkadian ruler Shar-Kali-Sharri, one of whose year names mentions the construction of two temples there. Babylon was ruled by an ensi (governor) for the empire. History →

The spelling Babylon is the Latin representation of Koine Greek Babylṓn (Βαβυλών), derived from the native Akkadian: 𒆍𒀭𒊏𒆠, romanized: Bābilim, lit. 'gate of the god(s)'. Names →

The site covers an area of about 1,000 hectares (3+3⁄4 mi2), with about 450 hectares (1+3⁄4 mi2) within the several kilometer (mile) long city walls, containing a number of mounds, the most prominent of which are Kasr, Merkes (13 meters; 43 ft above the plain), Homera, Ishin-Aswad, Sahn, Amran, and Babil.
